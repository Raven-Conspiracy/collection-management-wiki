## 4.1 NWP 2-01, Intelligence Support to Naval Operations

**Publication:** NWP 2-01, Intelligence Support to Naval Operations  
**Date:** November 2010  
**Public URL:** https://info.publicintelligence.net/USNavy-IntelSupportNavalOps.pdf  
**Naval Doctrine Reference:** https://irp.fas.org/doddir/navy/ndp2.htm

### Core Concept
NWP 2-01 is the primary Navy doctrine for intelligence support to naval operations. It establishes the N2 staff structure, the Maritime Operations Center (MOC) and Maritime Intelligence Operations Center (MIOC), numbered fleet intelligence roles, ONI's role, collection management authority in naval forces, and integration with the joint intelligence architecture.

### Naval Collection Management Authority

> "The intelligence staff develops more specific questions known as information requirements (those items of information that must be collected and processed to develop the intelligence required by the commander)." — NWP 2-01

> "A subset of information requirements that are related to and would answer a PIR are known as EEIs." — NWP 2-01

> "Since few collection requirements are met fully by organic assets alone, collection resources available at the theater and national level are normally tasked as well. To do this effectively, the intelligence staff must know the capabilities and limitations of available collection resources, must understand the requirements validation process to obtain desired collection approval, and must identify the collection resources that can contribute to fulfilling mission requirements." — NWP 2-01

### N2 Staff and Collection Management Roles

**Fleet N2 Responsibilities (General):**
> "Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, Seventh, and Tenth Fleet N2s are responsible for supporting the intelligence requirements of forces assigned in their areas of operations (AOs). Like the fleet commander N2/N39s, the numbered fleet N2s conduct liaison with their foreign counterparts, provide guidance on foreign disclosure of intelligence, validate subordinate RFIs, and establish fleet collection policies and guidance." — NWP 2-01

**Carrier Strike Group (CSG) N2:**
> "The CSG N2 is responsible to the strike group commander for all intelligence and cryptologic personnel assigned to CSG subordinate units. Key areas of responsibility include: administrative and functional control of fleet intelligence detachment (FID) personnel; generation of operational task (OPTASK) intelligence and cryptology supplements; PIR evaluation; support to operational planning; oversight of strike group indications and warning (I&W) and reporting; and development of the strike group's intelligence integration plan." — NWP 2-01

> "The assistant N2 and the CRC [Cryptologic Resource Coordinator] are responsible for collection management functions." — NWP 2-01

**Cryptologic Resource Coordinator (CRC):**
> "The numbered fleet cryptologist supports the MOC through the technical direction of subordinate task force cryptologic resource coordinators (CRCs) ensuring their organic cryptologic collection plans satisfy the numbered fleet commander's priority intelligence requirements (PIRs)." — NWP 2-01

### Maritime Operations Center (MOC)

The MOC is the Navy equivalent of the AOC — the primary C2 node for maritime operations. The MIOC is the intelligence support element embedded within the MOC.

### Maritime Intelligence Operations Center (MIOC)

> "The primary intelligence support mechanism for the MOC is the MIOC." — NWP 2-01

> "The MIOC is an all-source intelligence organization whose operations and schedule are driven by the needs of the commander and the established MOC battle rhythm. The MIOC is responsible for attaining, maintaining, and sharing intelligence-related situational awareness. Its primary function is to satisfy the commander and staff's requirements by planning, conducting, collecting, analyzing, and disseminating reliable and timely intelligence. These actions are centered on adversary intentions, I&W, IO, targeting, and assessment." — NWP 2-01

> "The most visible component of the MIOC is an I&W watch that is charged with building situational awareness of the adversaries' disposition and activities, conducting predictive analysis of his impending operations, and communicating that intelligence through reports and continuous input to the common operational picture." — NWP 2-01

> "The MIOC maintains elements that provide support to both the cells within the MIOC as well as the staff members and functions throughout the MOC organization, such as the special security functions and foreign disclosure functions." — NWP 2-01

### Ship-Level Intelligence (CVN SUPPLOT)

> "One of the primary data managers for the COP is the intelligence team aboard the CVN. In order to meet these requirements and those of the theater and national agencies, the ship's IO organizes the intelligence team into five functional cells: 1. SUPPLOT (all-source intelligence fusion center onboard the CVN); 2. Strike intelligence analysis cell (SIAC); 3. Mission briefing/debriefing; 4. SSES [Ship Signal Exploitation Space]; 5. Multisensor interpretation (MSI)." — NWP 2-01

### Office of Naval Intelligence (ONI)

> "ONI, located within the NMIC in Suitland, MD, is the nation's center of excellence for maritime-related intelligence. ONI's primary mission is to provide maritime intelligence to key strategic, operational, and tactical decision makers. This mission includes intelligence production on seaborne terrorism, weapons and technology proliferation, and narcotics and smuggling activities that directly supports joint warfighters, the Navy, and civil and national decision makers and agencies." — NWP 2-01

**ONI Support Capabilities Cited:**
- SPEAR/SWORD/SABER briefings (surface/submarine/amphibious threat briefs)
- Naval Intelligence Task Force-Maritime Security (NITF-MS)
- MIO (Maritime Interdiction Operations) briefings
- IO/C4ISR threat briefings
- ONI Detachment, Newport
- Cryptologic Services Group

> "ONI is the reachback center of excellence on submarine proliferation, acting collaboratively with maritime intelligence operations centers (MIOCs) and operational forces." — NWP 2-01

### Director of Fleet Intelligence (DFI)

> "The DFI's primary responsibility is assisting and advising all fleet commanders, NCCs, and Navy type commanders (TYCOMs) on ISR readiness issues as well as working to ensure intelligence readiness for maritime operations centers (MOCs)." — NWP 2-01

> "The DFI is tasked with the mission of optimizing intelligence readiness across all Navy mission areas and serving as the operating forces' advocate in the development, integration, and fielding of intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) systems." — NWP 2-01

### Naval ISR and Joint Architecture Integration

> "A truly joint intelligence infrastructure must be created to provide the best possible intelligence to the JFC. It must be constructed to ensure protection of information and intelligence from inadvertent disclosure and guarantee integrity of the data and assured access to all sources." — NWP 2-01

> "The JIOC is the focal point for the combatant command's intelligence planning, collection management, analysis, and production effort." — NWP 2-01

> "The DIOCC's primary functions are serving as the DOD lead for collection management and intelligence planning and providing support to combatant command JIOCs and JTFs." — NWP 2-01

## 4.2 NTTP 2-01.3 and Maritime IPB (MIPOE)

**Note on Availability:** NTTP 2-01.3 is a Navy Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures publication; it is controlled distribution but aspects of Maritime Intelligence Preparation of the Operational Environment (MIPOE) doctrine are accessible through NWP 2-01 and joint references.

**Maritime IPB (MIPOE)** applies the four-step IPB process to the maritime environment:

> "Intelligence preparation of the operational environment (IPOE) is the analytical process used by intelligence organizations to produce intelligence assessments, estimates, and other intelligence products in support of the commander's decision-making process. It is a continuous process that involves four major steps: defining the total operational environment, describing the impact of the operational environment, evaluating the adversary, and determining and describing adversary potential COAs." — NWP 2-01

> "The IPOE process assists commanders and their staffs in achieving information superiority by identifying adversary COGs, focusing intelligence collection at the right time and place, and analyzing the impact of the operational environment on military operations." — NWP 2-01
