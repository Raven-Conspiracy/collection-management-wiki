## 1. Doctrinal Definition

### Statutory Foundation

The authoritative, statutory definition of Geospatial Intelligence originates in **10 U.S.C. § 467**, enacted by the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 1997 (Pub. L. 104-201) and updated through subsequent legislation. The current text, accessible via the [Office of the Law Revision Counsel](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title10-section467&num=0&edition=prelim), reads:

> *"Geospatial intelligence means the exploitation and analysis of imagery and geospatial information to describe, assess, and visually depict physical features and geographically referenced activities on or about the earth. Geospatial intelligence consists of imagery, imagery intelligence, and geospatial information."*

This three-component statutory formula—imagery + IMINT + geospatial information—is the bedrock from which all US doctrine derives. The same statute defines each component term:

- **Imagery** — "a likeness or presentation of any natural or manmade feature or related object or activity and the positional data acquired at the same time the likeness or representation was acquired, including (i) products produced by space-based national intelligence reconnaissance systems; and (ii) likenesses or presentations produced by satellites, airborne platforms, unmanned aerial vehicles, or other similar means." *Expressly excluded*: handheld or clandestine photography taken by or on behalf of HUMINT collection organizations ([10 U.S.C. § 467(2)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title10-section467&num=0&edition=prelim)).

- **Imagery intelligence (IMINT)** — "the technical, geographic, and intelligence information derived through the interpretation or analysis of imagery and collateral materials" ([10 U.S.C. § 467(3)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title10-section467&num=0&edition=prelim)).

- **Geospatial information** — "information that identifies the geographic location and characteristics of natural or constructed features and boundaries on or about the earth," including data from remote sensing, mapping, and surveying technologies, and "mapping, charting, geomatics data, and related products and services" ([10 U.S.C. § 467(4)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title10-section467&num=0&edition=prelim)).

### JP 2-03 Codification

**Joint Publication 2-03, *Geospatial Intelligence in Joint Operations*** (5 July 2017) — the primary joint doctrine document for GEOINT — directly quotes the § 467 formula and expands it: "[GEOINT] collection encompasses all aspects of literal, infrared (IR), and synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imagery; overhead persistent IR capabilities; and geospatial information and services (GI&S). GEOINT includes the exploitation and analysis of electro-optical, IR, and radar imagery, as well as the exploitation and analysis of geospatial, spectral, laser, IR, radiometric, SAR phase history, polarimetric, spatial, and temporal data" ([JP 2-03, p. I-1](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf)).

**JP 2-0, *Joint Intelligence*** (2013/2022) — the keystone intelligence publication — lists GEOINT among the primary intelligence disciplines and cross-references JP 2-03 for functional detail ([JP 2-0 series, JCS.mil](https://www.jcs.mil/Doctrine/Joint-Doctrine-Pubs/2-0-Intelligence-Series/)). Appendix B of JP 2-0 catalogs intelligence disciplines; GEOINT is treated as the discipline that integrates the three statutory components.

### Critical Terminological Distinctions

The following distinctions, codified in [10 U.S.C. § 467](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title10-section467&num=0&edition=prelim) and elaborated in [TC 2-22.7 (Army GEOINT Handbook)](https://info.publicintelligence.net/USArmy-GeospatialIntel.pdf), are essential for precise usage:

| Term | What It Is | What It Is Not |
|---|---|---|
| **Imagery** | The raw collected product (the "picture") + positional data | The analysis of the picture |
| **IMINT** | The analytic *discipline* — interpretation of imagery and collateral materials | The raw imagery itself |
| **GI&S** | Geospatial Information and Services — maps, charts, geodesy, datums, elevation models | Either imagery or IMINT |
| **GEOINT** | The *discipline* encompassing all three; also used as shorthand for the resulting intelligence products | A single-source collection type |

TC 2-22.7 further clarifies that "GEOINT consists of any one or any combination of the following components: imagery, IMINT, or GI&S." The full intelligence value, however, derives from integration of all three ([TC 2-22.7, p. 1-1](https://info.publicintelligence.net/USArmy-GeospatialIntel.pdf)).

The [NGA GEOINT Basic Doctrine Publication 1-0 (2006, updated 2018)](https://irp.fas.org/agency/nga/doctrine.pdf) — the NGA's foundational doctrine document — further frames GEOINT as "layered data combined into a single product," with GI&S forming the georeferenced foundation over which imagery and IMINT products are stacked.

## 2. Functional Manager (ICD 113)

### ICD 113 and the Functional Manager Construct

**Intelligence Community Directive 113 (ICD 113), *Functional Managers*** (19 May 2009) — issued by the Director of National Intelligence — establishes the Functional Manager (FM) construct across the IC. An FM is responsible for the policy, oversight, coordination, and integration of a specific intelligence discipline community-wide. The ICD is available at the [DNI website](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/what-we-do/ic-related-menus/ic-related-links/intelligence-community-directives) and indexed at [FAS IRP](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/index.html).

Under ICD 113, **the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA) serves as the GEOINT Functional Manager for the Intelligence Community.** The Director of NGA (D/NGA) holds this role concurrently with the position of DoD GEOINT Manager under [DoDI 3115.15](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf).

### NGA's Dual Role

DoDI 3115.15, *Geospatial Intelligence (GEOINT)* (6 December 2011), provides the DoD policy implementation of ICD 113 as it applies to GEOINT. It explicitly assigns to "the Director, National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA), acting concurrently as the DoD GEOINT Manager and as the GEOINT Functional Manager pursuant to [ICD 113]" the comprehensive authority to issue strategic guidance, policies, and procedures for all GEOINT conducted under the Secretary of Defense's authority ([DoDI 3115.15, p. 3](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf)).

### Functional Manager Responsibilities

As GEOINT FM, the D/NGA's authorities and responsibilities include:

- **Community-wide policy and standards**: Issuing guidance and establishing interoperability standards that all IC components must follow for GEOINT systems and production.
- **Resource management**: Managing GEOINT resources, personnel, and programs within the National Intelligence Program (NIP) and Military Intelligence Program (MIP), and preparing consolidated NGA program and budget submissions to the USD(I&S) and DNI.
- **Collection management oversight**: Coordinating with Combatant Commands to synchronize DoD ISR resources and monitoring execution of GEOINT collection operations.
- **Advisory tasking**: Providing advisory tasking recommendations for Service-operated airborne and surface-based GEOINT collection platforms and sensors, as noted in [JP 2-03](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf).
- **Standards and interoperability**: Ensuring that systems producing or using GEOINT comply with NGA-established GEOINT standards ([DoDI 3115.15](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf)).
- **NST deployment**: NGA maintains National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency Support Teams (NSTs) embedded at each Combatant Command's Joint Intelligence Operations Center (JIOC) and at each Service and key agencies, providing on-site GEOINT expertise ([JP 2-03, p. II-2](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf)).

Military departments and Combatant Commands are obligated by DoDI 3115.15 to coordinate with D/NGA on all new GEOINT-related activities or programs, provide trained GEOINT personnel to NGA, and give the NSG broad access to theater-collected GEOINT data ([DoDI 3115.15](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf)).

## 3. Collection Authorities and Legal Anchors

### Statutory Authority

The primary statutory anchors for US GEOINT authority are found in **Title 10** (Armed Forces) and **Title 50** (War and National Defense) of the U.S. Code:

- **[10 U.S.C. § 441](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/10/441)**: Establishes NGA as "a combat support agency of the Department of Defense" with "significant national missions." The Director, NGA, is the agency head. The DNI retains authority to approve collection requirements levied on national imagery collection assets and resolve priority conflicts.
- **[10 U.S.C. § 442 et seq.](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=%2Fprelim%40title10%2FsubtitleA%2Fpart1%2Fchapter22&edition=prelim)**: NGA's Title 10 mission authorities — support to DoD, the joint force, and combat support functions.
- **50 U.S.C. § 3045**: NGA's Title 50 authority to support the geospatial intelligence requirements of the Department of State and other non-DoD departments and agencies.
- **[10 U.S.C. § 467](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title10-section467&num=0&edition=prelim)**: Definitions (see Section 1).

NGA holds both Title 10 and Title 50 authorities, making it unique among intelligence agencies in the breadth of its authorized customer base, as analyzed in [*A JA's Role at the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency*, The Army Lawyer (2019)](https://tjaglcs.army.mil/tal-2019-issue-2/Post/6034/A-JA-s-Role-at-the-National-Geospatial-Intelligence-Agency).

### National System for Geospatial Intelligence (NSGI / NSG)

The **National System for Geospatial Intelligence (NSG)** — sometimes styled NSGI in older doctrine — is the integrated framework of GEOINT collection, processing, exploitation, analysis, and dissemination capabilities across the US Government. NGA serves as the managing authority for the NSG strategy and is also a member of the broader **Allied System for Geospatial Intelligence (ASG)** with Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, and New Zealand ([NGA Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Geospatial-Intelligence_Agency)). [DoDI 3115.15](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf) requires Combatant Commands to provide the NSG with broad access to collected theater GEOINT data. The NSG Commercial GEOINT Strategy (2021) established priorities for unclassified and commercial sourcing ([Line of Departure, Army, 2024](https://www.lineofdeparture.army.mil/Journals/Warrant-Officer-Journal/Archive/July-September-2024/GEOINT-Production/)).

### United States Imagery and Geospatial Information System (USIGS)

The **USIGS** is the legacy enterprise architecture term for the classified system of record through which imagery and geospatial information are tasked, collected, processed, stored, and disseminated. Under USIGS architecture, NGA manages satellite collection requirements and develops distribution protocols ([JP 2-03, p. II-2](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf)).

### NRO Ownership of Overhead Platforms

The **National Reconnaissance Office (NRO)** designs, builds, and operates overhead intelligence collection satellites — the National Technical Means (NTM). NGA tasks NTM imagery via national channels through the DNI's collection tasking authority under [10 U.S.C. § 441(c)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/10/441). The NRO-NGA relationship is the backbone of national imagery collection: NRO collects, NGA exploits and disseminates, as described at the [Naval War College GEOINT/IMINT research guide](https://usnwc.libguides.com/c.php?g=494120&p=3381562).

### Commercial GEOINT Initiative — EOCL and Related Contracts

The US Government has dramatically expanded commercial satellite imagery acquisition. Key contracts include:

- **Electro-Optical Commercial Layer (EOCL)**: In May 2022, the NRO announced the "largest-ever commercial imagery contract effort," awarding contracts valued at approximately $4 billion over the following decade to **Maxar**, **Planet**, and **BlackSky** for commercial electro-optical imagery. These were effective 22 May 2022 with a five-year base and multiple one-year options through 2032 ([Geospatial World, May 2022](https://geospatialworld.net/blogs/nro-announces-billion-dollar-historic-commercial-imagery-contracts-to-maxar-planet-and-blacksky/)). The EOCL awards transitioned requirements from the prior EnhancedView Follow-On (EVFO) contract.

- **Global Enhanced GEOINT Delivery (G-EGD)**: An NGA-managed program (begun 2011) delivering unclassified imagery to over 400,000 US Government users. The program integrated Planet and BlackSky data in 2020, and by 2022 was incorporating SAR data from **Capella Space**, **ICEYE**, **Umbra**, and others ([Satellite Today, September 2022](https://www.satellitetoday.com/government-military/2022/09/20/nga-renews-maxars-g-egd-imagery-contract-for-third-option-year/)).

- **SAR and hyperspectral acquisitions**: As of 2023, the NRO held five SAR commercial contracts and had awarded six hyperspectral contracts, reflecting the community's broadening beyond EO imagery ([USGIF, May 2023](https://usgif.org/commercial-collaboration-nga-nro-discuss-commercial-imagery-ambitions/)).

### Advanced Geospatial Intelligence (AGI)

**Advanced Geospatial Intelligence (AGI)** is a publicly acknowledged GEOINT sub-discipline referenced in NGA's stated mission areas and in the [DoDI 3115.15 framework](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf). NGA research explicitly addresses "Advanced Phenomenologies" as a research thrust area, per [NGA STTR solicitation documents (August 2023, media.defense.gov)](https://media.defense.gov/2023/Aug/22/2003285635/-1/-1/0/OSD-NGA_STTR_23C.PDF). The specific technical content of AGI programs remains classified beyond publicly available references.

## 4. Primary Collection Methods / Sub-Disciplines

GEOINT encompasses a broad and expanding set of collection methods. [JP 2-03](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf) frames the collection taxonomy around "literal, infrared (IR), and synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imagery; overhead persistent IR capabilities; and geospatial information and services." Individual sub-disciplines are:

### EO (Electro-Optical) Imagery

EO imagery uses optical sensors to record reflected sunlight in the visible spectrum. **Panchromatic** sensors record a single broad visible band, yielding high-resolution black-and-white imagery optimized for geometric detail. **Multispectral** sensors record multiple discrete spectral bands (visible through near-infrared), enabling spectral discrimination of vegetation, soils, and surface materials. Commercial examples include Maxar's WorldView constellation (0.30 m panchromatic) and Planet's SkySat fleet (0.50 m), both now under EOCL ([Geospatial World, May 2022](https://geospatialworld.net/blogs/nro-announces-billion-dollar-historic-commercial-imagery-contracts-to-maxar-planet-and-blacksky/)). EO imagery is weather-dependent and ineffective at night without auxiliary illumination.

### IR (Infrared) Imagery

Infrared collection detects emitted or reflected IR energy. **Short-wave IR (SWIR)** penetrates haze and thin cloud cover. **Mid-wave IR (MWIR)** and **long-wave IR (LWIR)** — thermal sensors — detect heat emitted by objects, enabling detection of vehicle engines, people, and industrial heat signatures regardless of lighting conditions. JP 2-03 references "overhead persistent IR capabilities" as a distinct GEOINT collection element ([JP 2-03, p. I-1](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf)). NIIRS has a parallel infrared rating scale alongside the visible scale.

### SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar)

SAR is an active radar sensor that transmits microwave energy and records the reflected return. It operates day or night and penetrates cloud cover, making it all-weather capable. Primary imaging modes include:

- **Spotlight**: Dwells on a small area, maximizing resolution (typically sub-meter).
- **Stripmap**: Continuous strip collection along the flight path.
- **ScanSAR**: Wide-area swath collection at reduced resolution.

SAR also enables **Coherent Change Detection (CCD)**: by differencing two phase-coherent SAR collections of the same area, very subtle surface disturbances—footprints, vehicle tracks, disturbed soil, activity at a site invisible in optical imagery—become visible. BAE Systems describes CCD as providing "very useful and otherwise unavailable insights" for military C4ISR and other missions, noting that "CCD images are captured via radar, not photography; they are not affected by weather or darkness" ([BAE Systems CCD explainer](https://www.baesystems.com/en-us/definition/what-is-coherent-change-detection)). The NRO's EOCL expansion explicitly includes SAR data providers ([USGIF, May 2023](https://usgif.org/commercial-collaboration-nga-nro-discuss-commercial-imagery-ambitions/)).

### GMTI (Ground Moving Target Indicator)

GMTI uses Doppler radar techniques to detect and track objects moving across the ground (or at sea), discriminating movers from static clutter based on Doppler shift. Historically provided by the **E-8 JSTARS** (Joint Surveillance Target Attack Radar System) aircraft, which retired from operational service in 2023. The Space Force, in coordination with the NRO and NGA, is developing a space-based GMTI constellation with initial dedicated satellites expected in 2028 and operational deployment first to Indo-Pacific Command ([Defense One, August 2025](https://www.defenseone.com/defense-systems/2025/08/space-force-launch-ground-target-tracking-satellites-next-year/407208/)). GMTI straddles doctrinal lines: it is sometimes classified under GEOINT when the output is geospatially referenced tracking, and sometimes under MASINT or SIGINT when the focus is on signal characterization.

### Hyperspectral / Multispectral Imagery (HSI/MSI)

While multispectral imagery records a handful of discrete spectral bands, **hyperspectral imagery (HSI)** records hundreds of contiguous narrow bands across the visible and infrared spectrum. This enables **spectral signature matching**: identifying specific materials, camouflage coatings, vegetation health, soil disturbance, and even chemical residues based on their unique spectral fingerprints. NGA Research addresses "Advanced Phenomenologies" encompassing spectral analytics ([NGA STTR solicitation, 2023](https://media.defense.gov/2023/Aug/22/2003285635/-1/-1/0/OSD-NGA_STTR_23C.PDF)). The NRO awarded six hyperspectral commercial contracts in 2023 alone, indicating rapid expansion of this modality ([USGIF, May 2023](https://usgif.org/commercial-collaboration-nga-nro-discuss-commercial-imagery-ambitions/)).

### LIDAR

Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR) uses laser pulses to generate precise three-dimensional point clouds of terrain and structures. **Bare-earth LIDAR** penetrates vegetation to map ground elevation. **Foliage-penetrating** configurations detect objects beneath tree canopy that optical imagery cannot see. LIDAR data underpins digital elevation models (DEMs) and is referenced in [JP 2-03's](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf) description of GEOINT data as encompassing "spectral, laser, IR, radiometric" and spatial data.

### FMV (Full-Motion Video)

FMV is continuous video collected by airborne sensors — typically EO and IR cameras on systems such as the **MQ-9 Reaper** (equipped with the Multi-Spectral Targeting System, MTS-B class) and manned ISR platforms. AF DCGS exploitation systems process FMV from U-2, RQ-4, and MQ-9 platforms ([Air Force DCGS Fact Sheet, AF.mil](https://www.af.mil/About-Us/Fact-Sheets/Display/Article/104525/air-force-distributed-common-ground-system/)). FMV provides the "soda-straw" view — high-resolution coverage of a narrow area — but generates massive data volumes that strain exploitation capacity.

### WAMI (Wide-Area Motion Imagery)

WAMI uses large-format, high-megapixel focal plane arrays to capture motion imagery of an entire city-sized area simultaneously, enabling simultaneous tracking of hundreds of entities. The **Gorgon Stare** system, hosted on the MQ-9 Reaper, is the USAF's operational day/night WAMI capability. Increment 2 uses 368 five-megapixel sensors (ARGUS-IS technology from BAE Systems) to cover up to 100 km², outputting imagery at several terabytes per minute with up to 30 days of on-board archive ([Gorgon Stare Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gorgon_Stare); [SNC press release](https://www.sncorp.com/news-archive/sierra-nevada-corporation-achieves-milestone-for-usaf-s-advanced-wide-area-airborne-persistent-surveillance-system-gorgon-stare-increment-2/)). WAMI's extreme data volumes create profound PED scaling challenges (see Section 6).

### GI&S (Geospatial Information and Services)

GI&S encompasses maps, charts, geodetic data, datums, digital terrain/elevation data, and related products and services. GI&S is the geospatial foundation layer upon which imagery and IMINT products are georeferenced. Doctrine frames GI&S as "the foundation upon which all other information about the physical environment is referenced to form the common operational picture (COP)" ([TC 2-22.7, p. 1-4](https://info.publicintelligence.net/USArmy-GeospatialIntel.pdf)). NGA produces and maintains the US military's standard maps, nautical charts, aeronautical charts, and geodetic control.

## 5. Signature / Indicator Catalog — What GEOINT Sees

GEOINT derives intelligence value from what can be observed geospatially and spectrally. The following categories represent the unclassified tradecraft of "reading" imagery, drawn from [JP 2-03](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf), [TC 2-22.7](https://info.publicintelligence.net/USArmy-GeospatialIntel.pdf), and [CJCSI 3162.02](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/training/jts/cjcsi_3162_02.pdf):

### Order of Battle from Imagery

Systematic counting and identification of military equipment — aircraft, armor, artillery, naval vessels, missile launchers, and support vehicles — is a foundational IMINT task. Analysts use NIIRS (see Section 7) to determine whether the imagery resolution is adequate for the identification task. At NIIRS 4 (1.2–2.5 m GRD), analysts can "identify all large fighters by type" and "detect an open missile silo door." At NIIRS 6 (0.40–0.75 m), they can "distinguish between models of small/medium helicopters" and "detect wing-mounted stores" on bombers ([FAS NIIRS reference](https://irp.fas.org/imint/niirs.htm)).

### Activity Patterns: Revetments, Dispersal, and Ground Scarring

Construction of revetments, berms, and excavations around military equipment indicates hardening and dispersal operations. NIIRS 4-level imagery enables analysts to "detect recently constructed weapon positions (e.g., tank, artillery, self-propelled gun) based on the presence of revetments, berms, and ground scarring in vegetated areas" ([FAS NIIRS reference](https://irp.fas.org/imint/niirs.htm)). Periodic change detection reveals whether dispersal is defensive preparation or a prelude to offensive action.

### Construction and Bed-Down

New construction of hardened aircraft shelters (HAS), missile storage igloos, ammunition storage points, barracks, and support facilities is observable through sequential imagery comparison. Construction activity timelines inform estimates of when a capability will become operational.

### Infrastructure Damage and Battle Damage Assessment (BDA)

Post-strike imagery supports BDA as defined in **CJCSI 3162.02, *Combat Assessment*** (8 March 2019). NGA "provides timely, relevant, and accurate imagery, imagery intelligence, and geospatial information" in support of BDA, including "functional management and federated reach-back support to numerous joint targeting processes involving exploitation of GEOINT" ([CJCSI 3162.02, p. A-3](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/training/jts/cjcsi_3162_02.pdf)). The doctrinal BDA process has three phases: Phase 1 (Physical Damage/Change Assessment), Phase 2 (Functional Damage/Change Assessment), and Phase 3 (Target System Damage Assessment, led by DIA).

### Cratering Analysis

Imagery analysts examine the size, shape, depth signature, and pattern of craters to determine munitions type, quantity, and accuracy. Crater analysis is a core component of Phase 1 BDA. It also supports battle damage assessment under [JP 3-60 targeting doctrine](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/FOID/Reading%20Room/Joint_Staff/21-F-0520_JP_3-60_9-28-2018.pdf).

### Movement Detection

Static imagery can capture vehicles or forces caught during a collection pass. GMTI and WAMI provide *persistent* movement detection. WAMI systems like Gorgon Stare enable "real-time forensics capability to intelligence analysts that enables rapid adversary pattern-of-life analysis" ([RUSI WAMI Systems analysis](https://www.rusi.org/explore-our-research/publications/rusi-defence-systems/wide-area-motion-imagery-systems-evolution-capabilities-and-mission-sets)). A WAMI "time machine" capability allows analysts to rewind archived imagery to reconstruct the sequence of events around a significant incident.

### Camouflage, Concealment, and Deception (CCD) Defeat

Standard optical camouflage may defeat visible-band imagery but can be penetrated by SAR (which detects surface geometry and texture, not color), multispectral imagery (which detects unnatural spectral signatures), and SAR coherent change detection (which detects even subtle activity beneath or around camouflaged positions). [BAE Systems' CCD analysis](https://www.baesystems.com/en-us/definition/what-is-coherent-change-detection) notes that "radar-based CCD is not affected by weather or darkness." NIIRS 7-level visible imagery can "distinguish between tanks and three-dimensional tank decoys" ([FAS NIIRS reference](https://irp.fas.org/imint/niirs.htm)).

### Foliage Penetration

Airborne LIDAR penetrates forest canopy to generate bare-earth terrain models, revealing road networks, fortifications, and structures concealed beneath heavy vegetation. SAR at certain frequencies provides some foliage penetration capability. This is particularly relevant for detecting road traffic, logistics nodes, and fighting positions in forested terrain.

### Maritime Activity and Ship Identification

GEOINT provides comprehensive maritime intelligence. At NIIRS 3, analysts can "identify a large surface ship in port by type (e.g., cruiser, auxiliary ship, noncombatant/merchant)." At NIIRS 6, analysts can "identify individual launcher covers" on naval combatants. NGA provides "Maritime GEOINT" including ship identification, port activity analysis, and transit tracking, including in support of DHS border security missions ([NGA border protection GEOINT, ExecutiveGov, 2025](https://www.executivegov.com/articles/nga-border-protection-geospatial-intelligence)).

### Underground / Tunneling Indicators

Surface signatures of underground construction include spoil piles, disturbed soil, ventilation shaft heads, access portals, and characteristic vehicle traffic patterns around suspected entry points. Sequential change detection helps reveal the progress of underground construction even when the facility itself is not visible.

## 6. PED Chain

The Processing, Exploitation, and Dissemination (PED) chain converts raw GEOINT collection into finished intelligence. For GEOINT, the principal doctrinal reference is [JP 2-03, Chapter III](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf), supplemented by [DoDI 3115.15](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/311515p.pdf) and Service DCGS documentation.

### Tasking via NGA and USIGS / GEOINT Information Management Services (GIMS)

Collection tasking originates at the national level through NGA as GEOINT FM. GEOINT tasking involves "converting intelligence or mission requirements into collection requirements, establishing priorities, tasking or coordinating with appropriate collection sources or agencies, monitoring results, and re-tasking as required." JP 2-03 identifies **GEOINT Information Management Services (GIMS)** as the tasking system interface ([JP 2-03](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctorate/jp2_03(2017).pdf)). For national imagery (NTM), tasking flows through national channels to the NRO. For theater assets, Combatant Commands task through their GEOINT cells.

### Service DCGS Exploitation Architecture

The **Distributed Common Ground System (DCGS)** is the multi-Service ISR exploitation enterprise. Each Service operates a component:

- **Air Force DCGS (AF DCGS)** — the AN/GSQ-272 SENTINEL weapon system, designated the Air Force's primary ISR analysis and exploitation system. It processes data from U-2, RQ-4 Global Hawk, MQ-9 Reaper, and other ISR platforms. The **480th ISR Wing (480 ISRW)**, headquartered at Joint Base Langley-Eustis, Virginia, is "the AF DCGS lead wing and is responsible for executing worldwide AF DCGS operations" across 27 geographically separated, networked operational sites globally ([Air Force DCGS Fact Sheet, AF.mil](https://www.af.mil/About-Us/Fact-Sheets/Display/Article/104525/air-force-distributed-common-ground-system/); [GDIT AF DCGS case study](https://www.gdit.com/perspectives/case-studies/air-force-dcgs-global-joint-operations/)).

- **DCGS-Army (DCGS-A)** — "the Army's premier ISR enterprise for the tasking of sensors, analysis and processing of data, exploitation of data, and dissemination of intelligence (TPED) across all echelons." DCGS-A "connects tactical, operational, and theater-level commanders to hundreds of intelligence and intelligence-related data sources at all classification levels" ([DCGS Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributed_Common_Ground_System)).

- **DCGS-Navy (DCGS-N)** — naval imagery and GEOINT exploitation node.
- **DCGS-Marine Corps (DCGS-MC)** — marine GEOINT exploitation consistent with [MCWP 2-21 (Imagery Intelligence, Marines.mil)](https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/MCWP%202-21.pdf).

All DCGS components are interconnected through the **DCGS Integration Backbone (DIB)** — the common data infrastructure standard enabling discovery and sharing of data across the enterprise ([Collins Aerospace DCGS description](https://www.rtx.com/collinsaerospace/what-we-do/industries/military-and-defense/battle-management/dcgs)).

### NGA Exploitation

NGA is "the primary source for GEOINT analysis, products, data, and services at the national level" and maintains responsibility for "tasking imagery and geospatial information collection, processing raw data, exploiting geospatial information and imagery, analyzing information and intelligence, disseminating information and GEOINT to consumers" ([JP 2-03, p. II-1](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf)).

### WAMI and FMV PED Scaling Challenges

WAMI represents an existential challenge to the PED model. A 2012 RAND study cited in [*Air & Space Forces Magazine*](https://www.airandspaceforces.com/article/1012eye/) calculated that "naïvely multiplying the motion imagery crew size by the number of motion imagery spot equivalents" from WAMI sensors could "by 2015, require up to 117,000 personnel dedicated to motion imagery exploitation alone — one-third of the Active Duty Air Force." The RAND recommendation was automated target recognition to alert human analysts when eyes-on analysis is warranted. Gorgon Stare Increment 2 alone generates several terabytes of video output per minute ([Gorgon Stare Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gorgon_Stare)). This bandwidth and analytic-manpower problem drives significant investment in AI/ML-assisted PED.

### TPED Scaling Considerations

The broader TPED (Tasking, Processing, Exploitation, and Dissemination) bottleneck in GEOINT is well documented in unclassified literature. The Army's [*Line of Departure* article (2024)](https://www.lineofdeparture.army.mil/Journals/Warrant-Officer-Journal/Archive/July-September-2024/GEOINT-Production/) identifies that unclassified GEOINT production on NIPR and SBU-E channels is a DoD priority to reduce cross-domain limitations and enable data sharing with partner nations. AI, machine learning, and computer vision are being integrated into TPED workflows to address volume growth.

## 7. Exploitation Tradecraft (Unclassified, Doctrinal)

### NIIRS — National Imagery Interpretability Rating Scale

The **NIIRS** is a 10-level (0–9) scale developed by the Imagery Resolution Assessments and Reporting Standards (IRARS) Committee to define and measure image quality in terms of what an analyst can *do* with an image — not merely its resolution. The scale was introduced in 1974 and has been adapted for four imaging modalities: Visible, Radar, Infrared, and Multispectral. Full criteria for all levels are publicly available at [FAS/IRP](https://irp.fas.org/imint/niirs.htm).

| NIIRS Level | GRD (Visible) | Representative Task |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | N/A | Interpretability precluded by obscuration, degradation, or very poor resolution |
| **1** | >9.0 m | Detect a medium-sized port facility; distinguish runways from taxiways at a large airfield |
| **2** | 4.5–9.0 m | Detect large hangars; identify large phased-array radars; detect large non-combatant ships at a known port |
| **3** | 2.5–4.5 m | Identify wing configuration of large aircraft; identify a large surface ship by type; detect vehicle revetments at a ground forces facility |
| **4** | 1.2–2.5 m | Identify all large fighters by type; detect an open missile silo door; detect individual vehicles at a motor pool |
| **5** | 0.75–1.2 m | Distinguish between MIDAS and CANDID aircraft by refueling equipment; identify deployed tactical SSM systems by type; count all medium helicopters |
| **6** | 0.40–0.75 m | Distinguish between helicopter models; identify individual launcher covers on SLAVA-class vessels; detect wing-mounted stores on bombers |
| **7** | 0.20–0.40 m | Identify fitments and fairings on a fighter-sized aircraft; distinguish between a turreted tracked APC and a medium tank; detect a missile on an SA-2 launcher |
| **8** | 0.10–0.20 m | Identify rivet lines on bomber aircraft; identify a hand-held SAM; identify windshield wipers on a vehicle; identify limbs on an individual |
| **9** | <0.10 m | Differentiate cross-slot from single-slot heads on aircraft skin panel fasteners; identify vehicle registration numbers on trucks; identify individual spikes in railroad ties |

([FAS NIIRS Reference Guide](https://irp.fas.org/imint/niirs.htm); [Wikipedia NIIRS entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIIRS))

NIIRS is embedded in collection planning: requirements are stated in terms of the NIIRS level needed to perform the analytical task, and imagery systems are characterized by the NIIRS level they can deliver under defined conditions.

### BDA Imagery Reporting

BDA tradecraft is governed by [CJCSI 3162.02](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/training/jts/cjcsi_3162_02.pdf) and [JP 3-60, *Joint Targeting*](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/FOID/Reading%20Room/Joint_Staff/21-F-0520_JP_3-60_9-28-2018.pdf). The systematic analytical process involves:

1. **Preparation and planning**: Review target materials, understand JFC objectives, gather pre-strike imagery.
2. **Physical Damage Assessment (Phase 1 BDA)**: Assess physical changes to target elements using post-strike imagery — cratering, structural collapse, scorch/fire signatures.
3. **Functional Damage Assessment (Phase 2 BDA)**: Estimate recuperation time based on damage, considering target type.
4. **Target System Damage Assessment (Phase 3 BDA)**: Aggregate damage at the system level; conducted by DIA or the designated responsible producer.
5. **Re-attack recommendation**: Based on assessed progress toward targeting objectives ([CJCSI 3162.02, Enclosure C](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/training/jts/cjcsi_3162_02.pdf)).

NGA supports all phases, including facilitating release of GEOINT to allies and coalition partners for combined BDA operations.

### Coherent Change Detection (CCD) Tradecraft

CCD exploits the phase coherence of two SAR collections over the same area at different times. Subtle surface changes — boot prints, tire tracks, disturbed gravel, cleared brush — cause phase decorrelation detectable in the difference image. [BAE Systems' public explainer](https://www.baesystems.com/en-us/definition/what-is-coherent-change-detection) describes analytical applications including detection of activity around hidden facilities and monitoring of areas under camouflage. The technique is weather- and darkness-independent, complementing optical methods.

### Hyperspectral Material Identification

HSI analysis compares the spectral signature of an observed material against reference libraries. Camouflage netting, for example, may closely match surrounding vegetation in the visible spectrum but diverge sharply in near-infrared bands. Industrial processes leave characteristic spectral emissions. Material identification through spectral analysis is a core AGI tradecraft element referenced in NGA research publications.

### Activity-Based Intelligence (ABI)

**ABI** is a publicly documented analytic methodology — described in detail in the unclassified textbook *Activity-Based Intelligence: Principles and Applications* (Patrick Biltgen and Stephen Ryan, 2016) — built on four pillars: (1) *Georeference to discover* (spatiotemporal correlation drives discovery); (2) *Integration before exploitation* (data is integrated by time and location before analysis); (3) *Sequence neutrality* (no assumed starting point — any data point may be the key); (4) *Data neutrality* (no bias toward classified over unclassified data) ([NDU Press ABI article](https://ndupress.ndu.edu/Joint-Force-Quarterly/Joint-Force-Quarterly-77/Article/581866/activity-based-intelligence-revolutionizing-military-intelligence-analysis/)).

JP 2-03 explicitly notes that "technical advancements in structured observation management (SOM), object-based production (OBP), and activity-based intelligence (ABI) are also promoting the integration of intelligence data; improving the ability to discover, access, and use data; and creating efficiencies in analysis and production" ([JP 2-03, p. I-2](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf)).

### Pattern of Life Tradecraft

Pattern of Life (PoL) analysis uses temporal stacking of imagery, WAMI, FMV, and GMTI data to characterize the normal activity patterns of a facility, person, or entity. Anomalies — absence of activity, unusual vehicle visits, changed routines — trigger analytic attention. WAMI systems like Gorgon Stare are designed specifically for "real-time forensics capability to intelligence analysts that enables rapid adversary pattern-of-life analysis" ([SNC Gorgon Stare press release](https://www.sncorp.com/news-archive/sierra-nevada-corporation-achieves-milestone-for-usaf-s-advanced-wide-area-airborne-persistent-surveillance-system-gorgon-stare-increment-2/)).

### Counter-Deception in Imagery

Decoys and mockups are a centuries-old military deception technique. NIIRS 7-level visible imagery can "distinguish between tanks and three-dimensional tank decoys" ([FAS NIIRS reference](https://irp.fas.org/imint/niirs.htm)), but lower-resolution imagery may not. Multi-sensor cross-cueing is the primary counter-deception approach: a structure that looks like a SAM system in optical imagery but shows no thermal signature, no footprint disturbance in CCD, and no radar cross-section consistent with metallic hardware is likely a decoy. Analysts are trained to look for the *absence* of indicators (no tracks leading to tanks, no maintenance activity) as well as positive identifiers.

## 8. Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths

**Definitive, visually compelling products**: GEOINT provides direct, observable evidence. You saw it — a missile system, a construction site, a ship in port. This "show me" quality gives GEOINT a persuasive authority in analytic products and policy briefings that other INTs often lack.

**Broad area coverage from space**: A single satellite pass can image tens of thousands of square kilometers. Commercial constellations like Planet's Dove constellation provide daily global coverage at 3–5 m resolution ([NRO EOCL announcement, Geospatial World, 2022](https://geospatialworld.net/blogs/nro-announces-billion-dollar-historic-commercial-imagery-contracts-to-maxar-planet-and-blacksky/)), providing a persistent change-detection baseline at strategic level.

**Persistent surveillance capability**: WAMI and FMV enable sustained surveillance of specific areas with continuous or near-continuous coverage. WAMI's forensic rewind capability allows reconstruction of events retroactively — a unique intelligence advantage for post-event reconstruction and target development.

**All-weather and day/night capability (SAR/IR)**: SAR is completely weather-independent and operates at night, filling the gap left by optical systems. Thermal IR detects heat signatures at night and through smoke.

**Geospatial precision**: GEOINT products are georeferenced, meaning every observed feature or activity can be precisely located on the earth. This enables direct support to targeting (precise aimpoints), navigation, and route planning.

### Limitations

**Weather dependency (optical)**: Optical (EO) collection requires clear skies. Dense cloud cover, smoke, and dust degrade or eliminate collection. Targeting missions against cloud-covered areas must rely on SAR, revisit rates, or gap-fill from other sensors.

**Revisit rate for space-based assets**: National technical means (NTM) satellites have constrained revisit rates determined by orbital geometry, number of satellites, and competing collection requirements. Commercial constellations partially mitigate revisit gaps but at reduced resolution compared to NTM. The lag between a requirement and imagery collection can be minutes to days depending on asset availability and priority.

**Bandwidth and PED for FMV/WAMI**: As documented in the RAND study cited in [*Air & Space Forces Magazine*](https://www.airandspaceforces.com/article/1012eye/), WAMI data volumes are potentially unsustainable under human-analyst-centric exploitation models. Bandwidth requirements for downlinking WAMI data and streaming to exploitation nodes are enormous, creating potential bottlenecks in operationally demanding scenarios.

**Deception and denial**: Camouflage, decoys, underground facilities, and spoofing can reduce GEOINT effectiveness. A sophisticated adversary can present a false picture to imagery — false missile transporters, inflatable tank decoys, hardened underground storage. Multi-sensor cross-cueing and CCD reduce but do not eliminate this vulnerability.

**Darkness limitation for some sensors**: While SAR and thermal IR work at night, visible EO collection requires daylight or artificial illumination. Night operations leave exploitation gaps for optical systems.

**Survivability of airborne platforms in contested environments**: Airborne ISR platforms — U-2, RQ-4, manned ISR — face access and survivability challenges in integrated air defense environments. The shift to space-based alternatives (commercial, NRO) and the development of space-based GMTI ([Defense One, August 2025](https://www.defenseone.com/defense-systems/2025/08/space-force-launch-ground-target-tracking-satellites-next-year/407208/)) are partly driven by contested airspace concerns.

**National asset tasking lag**: For NTM collection, the approval process through national channels introduces a planning-to-collection lag. Time-sensitive targets may be displaced or concealed before collection occurs.

**Commercial release and classification rules**: NGA's G-EGD provides unclassified imagery to over 400,000 users ([Satellite Today, 2022](https://www.satellitetoday.com/government-military/2022/09/20/nga-renews-maxars-g-egd-imagery-contract-for-third-option-year/)). However, classification rules, foreign disclosure regulations, and "shutter control" provisions on commercial imagery can limit what can be shared with allies or released publicly. Balancing operational security with the increasing policy emphasis on unclassified production — as called for by the NSG Commercial GEOINT Strategy (2021) — is an ongoing doctrinal challenge ([Line of Departure, Army, 2024](https://www.lineofdeparture.army.mil/Journals/Warrant-Officer-Journal/Archive/July-September-2024/GEOINT-Production/)).

## 9. Cross-Cueing Patterns

Cross-cueing — the use of intelligence from one discipline to direct collection or analysis by another — is a force multiplier that dramatically increases the efficiency and effectiveness of the all-source intelligence enterprise. [JP 2-03](http://www.bits.de/NRANEU/others/jp-doctrine/jp2_03(2017).pdf) and [JP 2-0](https://www.jcs.mil/Doctrine/Joint-Doctrine-Pubs/2-0-Intelligence-Series/) both emphasize GEOINT's role within the multi-INT fusion architecture. Operational experience in large-scale combat operations training exercises has documented these patterns in unclassified literature.

### SIGINT → GEOINT (Emitter Location Cues Imagery)

SIGINT collection — particularly ELINT locating a radar emitter — provides a geographic "tip" that narrows the image analyst's search space from potentially thousands of square kilometers to a manageable sector. As documented in [*Army University Press* (2023)](https://www.armyupress.army.mil/Journals/Military-Review/Online-Exclusive/2024-OLE/Large-Scale-Combat-Operations-Targeting/), in 4th Infantry Division targeting operations: "Analysis of SIGINT emissions can greatly increase the effectiveness of other NTM collection efforts by focusing search areas into a manageable sector that can quickly cue other intelligence disciplines, thereby reducing the amount of time required to positively identify critical targets. This is especially true of GEOINT analysts who are typically 'snail trailing' through large images in search of possible targets." The SIGINT tip "does not completely remove the need to thoroughly exploit images, but it does provide a much shorter sensor-to-shooter timeline."

### GEOINT → SIGINT (New System Observed Cues ELINT Collection)

When GEOINT identifies a new or unknown emitter system at a facility — a new radar dish, a new antenna array, new hardstand with distinctive equipment — this observation cues SIGINT collection priorities. ELINT collectors are then directed to characterize the system's signal parameters. This bi-directional relationship is a core principle of multi-INT targeting development.

### HUMINT → GEOINT (Source Tip Cues Refresh)

A human source reporting activity at a specific location — construction, vehicle movements, personnel changes — triggers an imagery refresh request. GEOINT can then confirm, deny, or expand on the HUMINT report with observable evidence. The interaction between HUMINT reporting and GEOINT confirmation was famously illustrated in the Abbottabad compound analysis, where satellite imagery was used to corroborate HUMINT-derived indicators and assess anomalous compound features ([Australian Army GEOINT overview, COVE, 2023](https://cove.army.gov.au/article/overview-geospatial-intelligence-defence-applications)).

### GEOINT → HUMINT (Anomaly Cues Source Tasking)

Conversely, GEOINT analysis revealing an anomalous facility, construction project, or activity pattern can generate a priority intelligence requirement tasked to HUMINT collectors. The image analyst's observation drives source development and collection planning.

### MASINT → GEOINT (Technical Signature Cues Imagery Confirmation)

A MASINT indicator — a seismic signature suggesting underground construction, a nuclear or chemical effluent detected by overhead sensors, a specific radar cross-section — cues imagery collection focused on the suspected location. GEOINT then attempts to find the surface manifestations (access portals, spoil piles, ventilation shafts) of the phenomenon indicated by MASINT.

### GEOINT → Targeting (BDA, TST Development)

GEOINT's most direct operational product is targeting support. JP 2-03 describes GEOINT's role in "precisely locating activities and objects; enabling safe navigation over air, land, and sea; assessing and discerning the meaning of events; and providing context for decision makers." BDA imagery (see Sections 5 and 7) supports the combat assessment phase (Phase 6) of the [JP 3-60](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/FOID/Reading%20Room/Joint_Staff/21-F-0520_JP_3-60_9-28-2018.pdf) targeting cycle. **Time-Sensitive Targeting (TST)** development leverages WAMI and FMV to monitor a moving target until a strike window opens. Post-strike GEOINT supports re-attack recommendation decisions through the phased BDA framework of [CJCSI 3162.02](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/training/jts/cjcsi_3162_02.pdf).
