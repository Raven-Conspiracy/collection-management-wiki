## 1. Doctrinal Definition

### Primary Definitions

**JP 2-0 Definition (Authoritative Joint Doctrine)**

The Joint Publication 2-0, *Joint Intelligence* (22 October 2013), provides the canonical joint definition in its Glossary, Part II:

> "A category of intelligence derived from information collected and provided by human sources. Also called HUMINT. (JP 1-02. SOURCE: JP 2-0)"

JP 2-0 Appendix B expands on this with a more operational description:

> "HUMINT is a category of intelligence derived from information collected and provided by human sources. There are 15 defense HUMINT executors. Collectively, they are known as the Defense HUMINT Enterprise. The Defense HUMINT Enterprise is capable of providing full-spectrum HUMINT support to military operations. The Defense HUMINT Enterprise and its members also partner with multinational HUMINT elements during multinational operations."

*(JP 2-0, Appendix B, para. 2; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf))*

**FM 2-22.3 Definition (Army HUMINT Collector Doctrine)**

Field Manual 2-22.3, *Human Intelligence Collector Operations* (September 2006), provides the operationally granular Army definition:

> "HUMINT is the collection of information by a trained HUMINT collector (military occupational specialties [MOSs] 97E, 351Y [formerly 351C], 351M [formerly 351E], 35E, and 35F), from people and their associated documents and media sources to identify elements, intentions, composition, strength, dispositions, tactics, equipment, personnel, and capabilities. It uses human sources as a tool and a variety of collection methods, both passively and actively, to gather information to satisfy the commander's intelligence requirements and cross-cue other intelligence disciplines."

*(FM 2-22.3, para. 1-1; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf) and [https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%202-22.3%20%20Human%20Intelligence%20Collector%20Operations_1.pdf](https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%202-22.3%20%20Human%20Intelligence%20Collector%20Operations_1.pdf))*

**ICD 304 Definition (IC Policy)**

Intelligence Community Directive 304, *Human Intelligence* (6 March 2008), uses the ICD 1 definition:

> "Human Intelligence (HUMINT) — A category of intelligence derived from information collected and provided by human sources."

*(ICD 304, Definitions; available at [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf) and [https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD-304.pdf](https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD-304.pdf))*

**DoDD 5200.37 Definition (Defense HUMINT)**

DoD Directive 5200.37, *Defense Human Intelligence* (January 16, 2025), provides the most comprehensive DoD-level operational definition:

> "HUMINT — A category of intelligence derived from information collected and provided by human sources by trained and certified HUMINT collectors holding the mission and authority to collect such information using approved HUMINT collection methodology and Defense HUMINT authorities."

*(DoDD 5200.37, Glossary; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf))*

**FM 2-0 Army Intelligence Definition**

Field Manual 2-0, *Intelligence* (October 2023), carries forward the ADP 2-0 definition:

> "Human intelligence is the collection by a trained human intelligence collector of foreign information from people and multimedia to identify elements, intentions, composition, strength, dispositions, tactics, equipment, and capabilities (ADP 2-0)."

*(FM 2-0, para. 1-72; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf))*

### Plain-Language Explanation

HUMINT is the oldest and, in important respects, the most irreplaceable intelligence discipline. Where signals intelligence (SIGINT) intercepts communications and geospatial intelligence (GEOINT) captures physical activity, HUMINT derives information directly from people — through questioning, debriefing, liaison, and source relationships. Because it relies on trained collectors interacting with human sources, HUMINT uniquely accesses what is in a person's mind: intentions, plans, morale, decision-making processes, and knowledge of programs or activities that leave no electromagnetic or physical signature. The FM 2-22.3 definition is notable because it explicitly names the MOSs authorized to conduct HUMINT — a signal that only properly trained and certified personnel may conduct formal HUMINT collection operations. The DoDD 5200.37 definition, updated in 2025, additionally emphasizes legal authority ("holding the mission and authority") as a constitutive element of HUMINT, distinguishing it from casual information gathering or other human-derived activities conducted without HUMINT authority.

## 2. Functional Manager (ICD 113)

### Background: The Functional Manager Framework

Intelligence Community Directive 113, *Functional Managers* (effective 19 May 2009), implements Executive Order 12333, Section 1.3(b)(12)(A), which requires the Director of National Intelligence to designate Functional Managers for the major intelligence disciplines. ICD 113 defines the roles, responsibilities, authorities, and obligations of these Functional Managers.

*(ICD 113; available at [https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD_113.pdf](https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD_113.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/index.html](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/index.html))*

Per ICD 113, Section F.2, the Functional Manager:

> "...shall advise the DNI on interoperability with respect to information environments, enterprise architectures, and mission processes that support the operation of the function; establish consistency in tradecraft, training, and other areas within their respective function through the development of standards and procedures; and work to ensure compliance with DNI policies and priorities, as applicable."

The Functional Manager's authorities include prescribing training, tradecraft, reporting, and function-specific technical architecture standards; advising IC R&D activities; recommending services of common concern; and advising the DNI on strategic resource management. The Functional Manager is accountable for "continuously improving the performance of their function and for enhancing cross-function integration."

*(ICD 113, Section F.5; available at [https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD_113.pdf](https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD_113.pdf))*

### HUMINT Functional Manager: CIA / National HUMINT Manager

EO 12333, as amended by EO 13470 (2008), Section 1.3(b)(12)(A)(ii), explicitly designates:

> "The Director of the Central Intelligence Agency is designated the Functional Manager for human intelligence."

*(EO 13470, Section 1.3(b)(12)(A)(ii); available at [https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm](https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm))*

ICD 304, *Human Intelligence* (6 March 2008), operationalizes this role as the **National HUMINT Manager (NHM)**, with the Director of the Central Intelligence Agency serving as NHM. The NHM responsibilities, as established in ICD 304, Section E.2, include:

- Managing the national HUMINT collection capability in accordance with Presidential priorities and DNI guidance
- Issuing annually an integrated national HUMINT plan with associated goals and performance objectives
- Developing, promulgating, and overseeing implementation of **core common standards** for clandestine and overt HUMINT, including human source validation, training, intelligence collection requirements, evaluation, intelligence reporting, source description lexicon, cover support, and other HUMINT tradecraft practices
- Managing an interagency source registry, technology registry, and cover registry
- Coordinating and deconflicting HUMINT operations and activities across IC elements

*(ICD 304, Section E.2; available at [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf))*

### Defense Intelligence Enterprise Manager (DIEM) for HUMINT: DIA

For the Defense intelligence component, DoD Directive 5105.21, *Defense Intelligence Agency* (January 25, 2023), establishes the Director, DIA as the **Defense Intelligence Enterprise Manager (DIEM) for HUMINT**:

> "In a DIE management role, the Director, DIA is the DIEM for HUMINT, in accordance with DoDD S-5200.37 and DoDI S-5200.42."

*(DoDD 5105.21; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5105_21.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5105_21.pdf) and [https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodd/510521p.pdf](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodd/510521p.pdf))*

In the DIEM role, DIA coordinates and deconflicts HUMINT activities including those involving operational use of individuals, provides technical and enabling support, maintains a governance structure to resolve issues across the Defense HUMINT Enterprise, manages the Defense Clandestine Service, and synchronizes HUMINT activities across DoD to satisfy national requirements.

### Relationship Between NHM and DIEM

Where ICD 304 and ICD 113 establish overlapping or parallel authorities, ICD 304 explicitly states: "the National HUMINT Manager shall exercise those authorities and responsibilities under ICD 113." The DIEM for HUMINT coordinates with the NHM on IC-level matters, while managing Defense-specific HUMINT enterprise functions. This two-tier structure — CIA as national NHM and DIA as defense DIEM — reflects the dual national/defense character of the HUMINT mission.

## 3. Collection Authorities and Legal Anchors

### EO 12333: The Foundational Executive Authority

Executive Order 12333, *United States Intelligence Activities* (December 4, 1981), as amended most substantially by EO 13470 (July 30, 2008), is the primary executive authority governing all US intelligence collection, including HUMINT. Section 1.1 sets the overarching policy:

> "All means, consistent with applicable Federal law and with full consideration of the rights of United States persons, shall be used to obtain reliable intelligence information to protect the United States and its interests."

Section 1.1(b) establishes the legal constraint:

> "The United States Government has a solemn obligation, and shall continue in the conduct of intelligence activities under this order, to protect fully the legal rights of all United States persons, including freedoms, civil liberties, and privacy rights guaranteed by Federal law."

EO 12333 grants CIA authority to "collect (including through clandestine means), analyze, produce, and disseminate foreign intelligence and counterintelligence" (Section 1.7(a)(1)) and grants DIA comparable authority (Section 1.7(b)(1)). Military service intelligence elements may "collect (including through clandestine means), produce, analyze, and disseminate defense and defense-related intelligence and counterintelligence to support departmental requirements" (Section 1.7(f)(1)).

*(EO 13470 amending EO 12333; available at [https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm](https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm))*

### DoDD 5240.01: DoD Intelligence Activities

DoD Directive 5240.01, *DoD Intelligence and Intelligence-Related Activities and Defense Intelligence Component Assistance to Law Enforcement Agencies and Other Civil Authorities* (September 27, 2024), is the DoD-level policy directive governing all DoD intelligence activities, including HUMINT. It establishes the policy framework within which Defense HUMINT activities are conducted, assigns responsibilities to the USD(I&S), and provides the procedural basis implemented by DoD Manual 5240.01.

*(DoDD 5240.01; available at [https://www.esd.whs.mil/portals/54/documents/dd/issuances/dodd/524001p.pdf](https://www.esd.whs.mil/portals/54/documents/dd/issuances/dodd/524001p.pdf))*

### DoDD 5200.37: Defense HUMINT Authority

DoD Directive 5200.37, *Defense Human Intelligence* (January 16, 2025), establishes the policy framework for Defense HUMINT activities. Key policy provisions include:

- The Secretary of Defense authorizes CCMDs, Military Departments, and DIA to conduct Defense HUMINT activities
- The direction and control of Defense HUMINT activities are inherently governmental functions that only US Government civilian employees or military personnel will perform
- Defense HUMINT activities must be coordinated and deconflicted through the joint force counterintelligence and HUMINT staff element (J2X) or equivalent
- Only trained and certified HUMINT personnel assigned to organizations with a HUMINT mission may conduct Defense HUMINT activities

*(DoDD 5200.37, para. 1.2; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf))*

### The J-2X: Joint CI/HUMINT Staff Element

JP 2-01, *Joint and National Intelligence Support to Military Operations* (5 July 2017), describes the J-2X in Chapter II as the cornerstone HUMINT coordination structure at the joint force level:

> "Joint Force Counterintelligence and Human Intelligence Staff Element (J-2X). In coordination with the CCMD J-2, the JFC normally establishes a J-2X. This organization integrates human intelligence (HUMINT) and CI by combining the HUMINT operations cell, the task force counterintelligence coordinating authority (TFCICA), a HUMINT analysis and requirements cell, and a CI analysis cell, all of which comprise the J-2X... A J-2X is the HUMINT and CI focal point for the JFC. As the JFC's tasking authority for HUMINT and CI collection, the J-2X manages, coordinates, and deconflicts HUMINT and CI collection within the joint operations area."

The J-2X also "maintains the command source registry, deconflicts source matters, and performs liaison functions with external organizations." It must be located in a SCIF with secure communications architectures compatible with component HUMINT elements and other intelligence organizations.

*(JP 2-01, Chapter II, para. 5.b.(3); available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf))*

DoDD 5200.37 further specifies the Joint Staff J2X role: it coordinates and communicates Defense HUMINT issues on behalf of the Joint Staff; assists the DIEM for HUMINT in developing Defense HUMINT force sourcing solutions; develops and modifies joint HUMINT doctrine; and represents CCMD HUMINT requirements to IC-level HUMINT committee boards.

*(DoDD 5200.37, Section 3.2; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf))*

### Defense HUMINT Enterprise (DHE) and DIA

The **Defense HUMINT Enterprise** is defined in DoDD 5200.37 as "the collective of DoD organizations authorized to conduct Defense HUMINT activities." The Enterprise operates through **Defense HUMINT Executors (DHEs)** — senior intelligence officials designated by each DoD Component head with the mission to conduct Defense HUMINT activities. The DHE approves, executes, and supports Defense HUMINT activities; develops and maintains HUMINT policy, doctrine, and force structure; assesses the authenticity, reliability, and utility of sources; and establishes HUMINT collection priorities.

*(DoDD 5200.37, Sections 3.1 and Glossary; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf))*

### Tactical HUMINT: Army MI Bn HCTs and USMC HETs

**Army HUMINT Collection Teams (HCTs).** FM 2-0 states that "HUMINT capabilities consist of HUMINT collection teams, HUMINT operations cells, and assigned biometric collection equipment." The Army accomplishes its HUMINT mission through interrogations, source operations (recruited, non-recruited, and debriefing), and HUMINT collection support activities including screenings, liaisons, support to targeting, and DOMEX.

*(FM 2-0, para. 1-72; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf))*

At the Corps level, a Tactical HUMINT Battalion (formerly Tactical Exploitation Battalion) organizes CI and HUMINT collection into **CI/HUMINT Companies** with **Operational Management Teams (OMTs)** and **Operational Teams (OTs)**. Each OT typically includes a Warrant Officer CI/HUMINT Technician and CI Agents/Interrogators (MOSs 97B/E), tailored to the mission at 3:1, 2:2, or 1:3 (CI-to-interrogator) ratios. The **HUMINT TOC (H-TOC)** serves as the hub for all CI/HUMINT operations, managing mission tasking, source administration, and reporting flows to the Corps ACE.

*(FAS IRP Tactical HUMINT Battalion article; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/humint.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/humint.htm))*

**USMC CI/HUMINT Teams.** MCWP 2-14, *Counterintelligence* (2002), describes the Marine CI/HUMINT Company (CI/HUMINT Co) as organic to the intelligence battalion within each Marine Expeditionary Force (MEF). The CI/HUMINT Co consists of a headquarters section, a CI platoon (with four CI teams and a TSCM team), an interrogator-translator (IT) platoon (with six IT teams), and **two to five HUMINT Support Teams (HSTs)**. The HST normally consists of one CI officer, two CI enlisted specialists, and three enlisted interrogator-translators, and supports the MAGTF by exploiting HUMINT and CI collection opportunities and providing tailored support to subordinate elements.

*(MCWP 2-14, para. 3003; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf))*

MCWP 2-14 notes that Marine HUMINT "operations cover a wide range of activities encompassing reconnaissance patrols, aircrew reports and debriefs, debriefing of refugees, interrogations of prisoners of war, and the conduct of CI force protection source operations." The MAGTF publication also notes: "Due to the intelligence requirements of commanders in direct contact with hostile forces, the line between CI and HUMINT at the tactical level is blurred almost beyond differentiation."

*(MCWP 2-14, para. 6002–6003; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf))*

### Title 10 vs. Title 50: High-Level Distinction

Title 10 of the US Code governs the armed forces — their organization, training, and equipping — and is the statutory basis for military operations. Title 50 governs war and national defense, including intelligence activities and covert action authorities. At the strategic and policy level, this distinction matters to HUMINT because:

- **Military HUMINT** conducted as part of military operations (interrogation of EPWs, debriefing of tactical sources, liaison) is generally understood as a Title 10 function — part of the Commander's authority over military forces.
- **Clandestine intelligence collection** — including recruited source operations and covert action — is generally governed under Title 50 authorities, requiring Presidential finding, Congressional notification, and specific approvals.

EO 12333, Section 1.7(a)(4), explicitly reserves covert action authority to the CIA absent a Presidential determination. Modern legal analysis (such as [Harvard National Security Journal, Vol. 3, "Demystifying the Title 10-Title 50 Debate"](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/nsj/2011/12/demystifying-the-title-10-title-50-debate-distinguishing-military-operations-intelligence-activities-covert-action/)) holds that Titles 10 and 50 "create mutually supporting, not mutually exclusive, authorities." Operations conducted under military command and control pursuant to a Secretary of Defense-issued execute order are military operations regardless of the collection method used.

The operational implication for HUMINT practitioners is that coordinating clandestine HUMINT activities between the military and CIA requires careful deconfliction under ICD 310, *Coordination of Clandestine Human Source and Human-Enabled Foreign Intelligence Collection and Counterintelligence Activities Outside the United States*, which implements EO 12333 Section 1.3(b)(20).

*(ICD 310; available at [https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD-310-Coord-of-Clandestine-Human-and-Human-enabled-FI-and-CI-outside-the-US-2016-06-27.pdf](https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD-310-Coord-of-Clandestine-Human-and-Human-enabled-FI-and-CI-outside-the-US-2016-06-27.pdf))*

## 4. Primary Collection Methods / Sub-Disciplines

FM 2-22.3 identifies the full spectrum of HUMINT collection activities:

> "HUMINT collection activities include these categories: tactical questioning, screening, interrogation, debriefing, liaison, human source contact operations (SCOs), document exploitation (DOCEX), and captured enemy equipment (CEE) operations."

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 1; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf))*

Additionally, FM 2-0 notes that Army HUMINT accomplishes its mission through "interrogations, source operations (recruited, nonrecruited, and debriefing), and HUMINT collection support activities (screenings, liaisons, support to targeting, and DOMEX)."

*(FM 2-0, para. 1-72; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf))*

### Source Operations / Controlled Source Operations

**Source Operations** (also called Human Source Contact Operations or SCOs) are, per FM 2-22.3, "operations directed toward the establishment of human sources who have agreed to meet and cooperate with HUMINT collectors for the purpose of providing information." These operations require specific authority — only designated and trained personnel "with the source operations mission may develop information through the direct and indirect questioning of overt or clandestine sources." Such personnel "operate under the authority and direction of a designated defense HUMINT executor."

*(JP 2-0, Appendix B, para. 2.b; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf))*

Within the MAGTF, MCWP 2-14 identifies **CI Force Protection Source Operations (CFSO)** as "overt source collection activities of an expedient nature intended to identify threats to the command in support of the commander's force protection mission."

*(MCWP 2-14, Chapter 7; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf))*

### Debriefing

JP 2-0 Appendix B defines debriefing as "the process of questioning cooperative human sources to satisfy intelligence requirements, consistent with applicable law. The source usually is not in custody and usually is willing to cooperate." Debriefings may be conducted at all echelons and operational environments.

JP 2-0 identifies categories of debriefing sources:
- **Friendly forces personnel**: combat patrols, pilots and crew, long-range surveillance teams, and SOF with information about the adversary or operational environment
- **Refugees/displaced persons**: particularly those from enemy-controlled areas or with access through employment
- **Returnees**: returned POWs, defectors, freed hostages, and MIA personnel
- **Volunteers**: those who freely offer information of intelligence value

*(JP 2-0, Appendix B, paras. 2.c.(1)–(4); available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf))*

### Interrogation

FM 2-22.3 defines interrogation as:

> "The systematic effort to procure information to answer specific collection requirements by direct and indirect questioning techniques of a person who is in the custody of the forces conducting the questioning."

Key doctrinal elements:
- Interrogation **may only** be conducted by personnel trained and certified in the interrogation methodology (MOSs 97E, 351M/351E, or others approved by DoD policy)
- Interrogations **are always** to be conducted in accordance with the Law of War, regardless of echelon or operational environment
- JP 2-0 states that "trained interrogators with current certification operating under DOD authority are permitted to conduct intelligence interrogations"
- Interrogation sources range from totally cooperative to highly antagonistic; all approaches must comply with the Detainee Treatment Act and applicable DoD directives

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 8; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf); JP 2-0, Appendix B, para. 2.a; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_0.pdf))*

### Liaison

FM 2-22.3 defines liaison operations as "programs to coordinate activities and exchange information with host country and allied military and civilian agencies and NGOs." Liaison is both a collection method and a coordination mechanism — it enables HUMINT collectors to develop relationships with host-nation security services, intelligence agencies, allied counterparts, and other organizations that may hold information relevant to collection requirements. MCWP 2-14 includes liaison as one of the CI collection activities: "conduct liaison with unit, JTF, other services, allied, and host nation intelligence and local intelligence, CI, and law enforcement agencies as appropriate."

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 1; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf); MCWP 2-14; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf))*

### Document and Media Exploitation (DOMEX) — HUMINT-Adjacent

ICD 302, *Document and Media Exploitation* (July 6, 2007), defines DOMEX as:

> "The processing, translation, analysis, and dissemination of collected hard copy documents and electronic media, which are under the U.S. Government's physical control and are not publicly available."

ICD 302 explicitly links DOMEX to HUMINT: "DOMEX activities will support a wide range of intelligence activities, including all source analysis, open source exploitation, Human Intelligence, Signals Intelligence, Geospatial Intelligence, and Measurement and Signature Intelligence."

*(ICD 302; available at [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-302.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-302.pdf))*

FM 2-22.3 lists DOCEX (document exploitation) as a HUMINT collection activity: "the systematic extraction of information from open, closed, published, and electronic source documents." DOMEX/DOCEX is considered HUMINT-adjacent because it typically relies on material initially obtained through HUMINT collection channels — seized or surrendered documents, captured media, and materials found on detainees. At the national level, the National Media Exploitation Center (NMEC), an IC center with DIA as executive agent, coordinates IC-wide DOMEX.

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 1; ICD 302; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-302.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-302.pdf))*

### Tactical Questioning vs. Interrogation

This is one of the most operationally significant distinctions in FM 2-22.3:

| Dimension | Tactical Questioning | Interrogation |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | "Expedient initial questioning for information of immediate tactical value" | "The systematic effort to procure information to answer specific collection requirements by direct and indirect questioning techniques of a person who is in the custody of the forces conducting the questioning" |
| **Who may conduct** | "Generally performed by members of patrols, but can be done by any DOD personnel" | "May only be conducted by personnel trained and certified in the interrogation methodology (MOSs 97E, 351M/351E, or select others as may be approved by DOD policy)" |
| **Source status** | Does not require custodial relationship; expedient and immediate | Requires that the person be "in the custody of the forces conducting the questioning" |
| **Purpose** | Immediate tactical value; fills out capture tag; answers JUMPS (job, unit, mission, priority questions, supporting information) | Answers specific collection requirements at all echelons |
| **Legal standards** | Any DoD personnel may ask questions lawfully; Law of War applies | Always conducted in accordance with Law of War; DoDD 3115.09 governs intelligence interrogation |

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 1 and FM 2-91.6; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-91-6.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-91-6.pdf))*

## 5. Signature / Indicator Catalog — What HUMINT Sees

HUMINT uniquely accesses categories of information that are substantially inaccessible or unreachable through technical collection disciplines. The Commander's Handbook for Persistent Surveillance (JCS, 20 June 2011) captures this in a single doctrinal statement:

> "Human Intelligence (HUMINT) can provide intent but may not be able to fix a target to a precise location."

*(JCS Persistent Surveillance Handbook, p. 3-1; available at [https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/pams_hands/surveillance_hbk.pdf](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/pams_hands/surveillance_hbk.pdf))*

JP 2-01 reinforces this in its discussion of collection management:

> "Collection managers are advised to match collection resources to the type of requirements and information gaps that are most likely to be satisfied by a particular collection operation (e.g., HUMINT and/or SIGINT can capture enemy intent, but GEOINT cannot)."

*(JP 2-01, Chapter III, para. 9.b.(3); available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf))*

The FAS IOSS threat intelligence overview (drawing on IC reference materials) articulates the indicator categories that HUMINT uniquely provides:

> "Human collectors can provide key insights into the intentions of an adversary, whereas technical collection systems are often limited to determining capabilities. HUMINT can be used to reveal adversary plans and intentions, or uncover scientific and weapons developments before they are used or are detected by technical collection systems. HUMINT can also provide documentary evidence such as blueprints of facilities, copies of adversary plans, or copies of diplomatic or policy documents."

*(FAS IOSS, "Intelligence Collection Activities and Disciplines"; available at [https://irp.fas.org/nsa/ioss/threat96/part02.htm](https://irp.fas.org/nsa/ioss/threat96/part02.htm))*

FM 2-22.3 specifies that HUMINT is used "to identify elements, **intentions**, composition, strength, dispositions, tactics, equipment, personnel, and capabilities" — with intent listed first and separately from the capability categories.

*(FM 2-22.3, para. 1-1; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf))*

**The HUMINT indicator catalog includes the following categories, derived from doctrine:**

**Intent and Plans-and-Intentions:**
HUMINT is the primary — sometimes only — source for indicators of adversary intent. A decision-maker's intentions may not manifest as physical activity, communications traffic, or observable signatures until execution. HUMINT sources with access to leadership deliberations, planning sessions, or internal decision documents can reveal intent before any other discipline detects execution preparation. FM 2-22.3's HUMINT intent diagram explicitly shows HUMINT's role as "shaping blue's visualization of red" through determination of capabilities, order of battle, vulnerabilities, and *intentions*.

**Decision-Maker Psychology and Leadership Dynamics:**
HUMINT collectors and debriefers elicit assessments of key leader personality, decision-making style, health status, stress indicators, and susceptibility to external influence. These psychological indicators are entirely inaccessible to SIGINT and GEOINT. Awareness of a foreign leader's medical condition, personal concerns, or internal political pressures constitutes information that technical disciplines cannot provide.

**Internal Political Dynamics:**
HUMINT can illuminate power struggles within foreign governments, factions within military organizations, relationships between civilian and military leadership, and conditions within bureaucracies. These soft indicators — morale, factional alignment, patronage networks — are invisible to technical collection.

**Morale and Will to Fight:**
Interviews with refugees, displaced persons, and returning personnel (all enumerated in JP 2-0) provide indicators of adversary unit cohesion, logistics sufficiency, and will to continue fighting — critical pre-battle and mid-battle intelligence unavailable through technical means.

**Contingency Planning and Course-of-Action Thinking:**
Human sources with access to planning staffs or decision forums can identify adversary COAs under consideration, including options that will never manifest in communications traffic or observable preparations unless executed. This allows friendly forces to anticipate branch and sequel options.

**Deception Narratives:**
HUMINT is both a target and a detector of adversary deception programs. A source with access to an adversary deception staff can reveal the narrative being fed to other collection disciplines — providing the key to interpreting ambiguous SIGINT or GEOINT data. Conversely, HUMINT sources can themselves be vectors for deception and require counter-deception analysis.

**Weapons Program Insider Knowledge:**
WMD programs, advanced weapons systems, and classified R&D activities may leave minimal external signatures during development. HUMINT sources with direct access to programs can report on program status, location, key personnel, production capacity, and timeline — information that technical signatures alone cannot confirm.

**Network Identification:**
Human sources identify personnel, relationships, organizational hierarchies, and financial flows within adversary networks. This personality intelligence feeds targeting and CI databases and enables link analysis that SIGINT and GEOINT can corroborate but typically cannot initiate.

## 6. PED Chain

The HUMINT Processing, Exploitation, and Dissemination (PED) chain converts raw information obtained from human sources into finished intelligence available to commanders and analysts.

### Source Validation and Vetting

DoD Instruction S-5200.42 (declassified portions cited through public DoDD summaries) establishes that "continuous vetting of sources shall be conducted to ensure information security, credibility, and reliability." The DHE is responsible for assessing "the authenticity, reliability, and utility of sources under their purview."

ICD 304 establishes that the National HUMINT Manager is responsible for developing and overseeing "core common standards for clandestine and overt HUMINT, including **human source validation**" as a specifically named area.

*(ICD 304, Section E.2.e; available at [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf); DoDD 5200.37; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf))*

Source validation in practice involves:
- Assessing a source's **placement and access** — does the source actually have access to the information they claim?
- **Screening** at the point of initial contact — FM 2-22.3 defines screening as "the process of identifying and assessing the areas of knowledge, cooperation, and possible approach techniques for an individual who has information of intelligence value"
- Ongoing **reliability and credibility assessment** throughout the source relationship
- Registration in the **defense source registration system of record**, required by DoDD 5200.37 for all HUMINT sources including interrogation sources

### HUMINT Reporting: The IIR

The primary HUMINT reporting vehicle is the **Intelligence Information Report (IIR)**. FM 2-22.3 describes the IIR at paragraph 10-6:

> "The IIR is used to report all HUMINT information in response to collection requirements. It is used to expand on information previously reported by a SALUTE report or to report information that is either too extensive or not critical enough for SALUTE reporting. IIRs are written at any echelon and 'released' by the appropriate authority before they enter the general Intelligence Community. Normally the G2X will be the release authority for IIRs."

The IIR workflow:
1. The HUMINT collector completes the IIR, including a source description and context statement
2. The **Team Leader** reviews the IIR and places a copy in the source or detainee local file
3. The **OMT (Operations Management Team)** reviews, requests additional information as necessary, adds administrative detail, and forwards to the HOC (HUMINT Operations Cell)
4. The **2X reviews and releases** the report into intelligence community channels

For tactical time-sensitive information, FM 2-22.3 notes that IIR text "can be forwarded to the unit's analytical elements and when it contains critical time-sensitive information, such as an impending attack, it is sent to units which may be affected by the information; however, it must be clearly marked 'unevaluated information, not finally evaluated intelligence.'"

*(FM 2-22.3, paras. 10-6 through 10-9; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf) and [https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%202-22.3%20%20Human%20Intelligence%20Collector%20Operations_1.pdf](https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%202-22.3%20%20Human%20Intelligence%20Collector%20Operations_1.pdf))*

DoD Instruction S-5200.42 (cited in public DoDD references) establishes that "information of intelligence value obtained in the course of Defense HUMINT and related intelligence activities shall be disseminated in IIRs." For tactical military operations, tactical IIRs (TIIRs) are submitted using US Joint Forces Command instructions; information of enduring intelligence value from TIIRs must also subsequently be reported in standard IIR format. IIR source descriptions and context statements must be reviewed by collectors "to ensure they do not compromise the source."

At the national level, HUMINT IIRs are entered into databases including INTELINK and disseminated to intelligence users across the community. The Khobar Towers investigation report notes that HUMINT IIRs were entered "in two databases — INTELINK and the DIA Support to Analysts File Environment (SAFE) system — for intelligence users in the field," with the unified command Joint Intelligence Center (JIC) responsible for disseminating HUMINT-derived intelligence up and down echelons of command.

*(FAS IRP, Khobar Towers Part 2; available at [https://irp.fas.org/threat/khobar_af/part2ae.htm](https://irp.fas.org/threat/khobar_af/part2ae.htm))*

### DIA HUMINT Online Tasking and Reporting (HOT-R)

At the unclassified public level, DIA's HUMINT career field descriptions reference a comprehensive automated HUMINT management and reporting infrastructure. While the specific HOT-R system details are not publicly documented at an unclassified level in current publications, DIA acknowledges that its HUMINT Career Field includes "management of the collection and reporting cycle." The Defense HUMINT Enterprise relies on automated source administration and reporting architectures: DoDD 5200.37 Section 2.2(e)(10) requires DIA to "establish, maintain, and ensure DIE access to a single, secure, and automated source administration information technology architecture to manage DoD and interagency de-confliction and coordination for all DoD human sources."

*(DoDD 5200.37, Section 2.2(e)(10); available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf); DIA HUMINT Career Field; available at [https://www.dia.mil/Careers/Career-Fields/Human-Intelligence/](https://www.dia.mil/Careers/Career-Fields/Human-Intelligence/))*

### HUMINT-to-All-Source Integration

JP 2-01 describes the exploitation pathway: "Exploitation of HUMINT is conducted by the JIOC and joint force analytical and/or production activities. This activity primarily involves analyzing HUMINT reporting for inclusion in all-source production and for database maintenance, and supports HUMINT and CI targeting and source validation."

*(JP 2-01, Chapter III, para. 15; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf))*

FM 2-0 articulates the integration principle: "Collection from the various intelligence disciplines is integrated during collection management to ensure a multidiscipline approach, which supports effective intelligence analysis, and, ultimately, all-source intelligence. In turn, all-source intelligence facilitates accurate situational understanding, decision making, and support to targeting."

*(FM 2-0, para. 1-65; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2_0.pdf))*

ICD 304 requires that "intelligence obtained from IC HUMINT operations or activities shall be disseminated, pursuant to DNI policy, to the maximum extent possible, with due regard for the protection of sources and methods." This maximum dissemination requirement drives HUMINT reports into all-source analyst databases.

*(ICD 304, Section D.8; available at [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf))*

## 7. Exploitation Tradecraft (Unclassified, Doctrinal)

### Source Validation Criteria

FM 2-22.3 describes the HUMINT collector's obligation during every collection session:

> "He must concentrate on the information being provided by the source and be constantly evaluating the information for both value and veracity based on collection requirements, current intelligence, and other information obtained from the source. Simultaneously, he must be alert not only to what the source says but also to how it is said and the accompanying body language to assess the source's truthfulness, degree of cooperation, and current mood."

The three-part assessment framework derived from FM 2-22.3 is:
1. **Placement and access**: Does the source have actual, direct, or indirect access to the information they claim?
2. **Reliability**: Based on past reporting history, how consistently accurate has the source been?
3. **Credibility**: Does the current report's content cohere with other known information, and is it internally consistent?

Screening operations — "the process of identifying and assessing the areas of knowledge, cooperation, and possible approach techniques for an individual who has information of intelligence value" — establish the initial baseline. "Indicators and discriminators used in screening can range from general appearance, possessions, and attitude to specific questions to assess areas of knowledge and degree of cooperation."

*(FM 2-22.3; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf))*

### Reliability and Credibility Scales (A–F / 1–6)

FM 2-22.3 Appendix B establishes the standard US military source-and-information evaluation matrix — the "Admiralty Code" or "NATO Intelligence Grading System" — used for all IIR source and information evaluations. This system uses two independent dimensions rated separately:

**Source Reliability (A–F):**

| Rating | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Reliable | No doubt about the source's authenticity, trustworthiness, or competency. History of complete reliability. |
| **B** | Usually reliable | Minor doubts. History of mostly valid information. |
| **C** | Fairly reliable | Doubts. Has provided valid information in the past. |
| **D** | Not usually reliable | Significant doubts. Has provided valid information in the past but inconsistently. |
| **E** | Unreliable | Lacks authenticity, trustworthiness, and competency. History of invalid information. |
| **F** | Reliability unknown | Insufficient information to evaluate reliability. No history to judge. |

**Information Credibility (1–6):**

| Rating | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Confirmed by independent sources | Logical, consistent with other relevant information, confirmed by independent sources. |
| **2** | Probably true | Logical, consistent with other relevant information, not confirmed. |
| **3** | Possibly true | Reasonably logical, agrees with some relevant information, not confirmed. |
| **4** | Doubtfully true | Not logical but possible, no other information on the subject, not confirmed. |
| **5** | Improbable | Not logical, contradicted by other relevant information. |
| **6** | Cannot be judged | The validity of the information cannot be determined. |

A rating of **A1** indicates the highest-confidence report (completely reliable source, confirmed by independent sources). A rating of **F6** indicates a new source with no track record reporting information that cannot be independently evaluated. An **E1** rating would indicate an unreliable source whose specific report has been independently confirmed — meaning the information itself may still be acted upon.

*(FM 2-22.3, Appendix B; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf); see also Wikipedia article on Intelligence source and information reliability for the full matrix: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence_source_and_information_reliability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence_source_and_information_reliability))*

### Corroboration Requirements

FM 2-22.3 explicitly addresses single-source risk:

> "Normal policy of not relying on single-source information should help prevent deceptions of this type from being effective."

JP 2-01 reinforces this principle from the collection management perspective: "Collection gathered from additional disciplines is often necessary to corroborate or increase friendly force confidence in gathered intelligence. Each discipline's limitations can be mitigated by the capabilities of the others, as different systems provide additional, and alternative, insights into the requirement."

*(JP 2-01, Chapter III, para. 9.b.(3); available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf))*

Naval Intelligence doctrine (NDP 2) articulates the analytical principle: "To preclude being deceived by our own analytical errors or by adversary deception, we use an all-source technique that permits the development of corroborating data. Further, all-source analysis can disclose conflicting data, which demand additional analysis to discern a more accurate picture."

*(NDP 2, Naval Intelligence; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/navy/ndp2.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/navy/ndp2.htm))*

### Reporting Standards (IIR Format, Sourcing Language)

FM 2-22.3 describes the five phases of every HUMINT collection session, with reporting as the final phase:

> "During the reporting phase, the HUMINT collector writes, edits, and submits written, and possibly oral, reports on information collected in the course of a HUMINT collection effort. These reports will be reviewed, edited, and analyzed as they are forwarded through the appropriate channels."

The IIR includes multiple report types beyond the standard IIR:
- **SALUTE reports** (for immediate tactical information)
- **Basic Source Data (BSD) reports** (source background)
- **Contact reports**
- **Screening reports**
- **Knowledgeability Briefs (KBs)**
- **Notice of Intelligence Potential (NIP)**
- **Lead Development Reports (LDRs)**
- **Interrogation summaries and plans**

Sourcing language in IIRs must be precise and not compromise source identity. Collectors "shall review details provided in IIR source descriptions and context statements to ensure they do not compromise the source."

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 10; DoDI S-5200.42 public summary; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf))*

Discredited or fabricated reporting must be formally addressed: DoD Instruction S-5200.42 (via DoDD 5200.37 references) requires that "intelligence information determined subsequent to publication to be unreliable due to fabrication or otherwise discredited sourcing shall be formally recalled or revised."

### Counter-Deception Considerations

FM 2-22.3 addresses the deception problem directly: documents and other materials "can be deliberately prepared to fall into enemy hands as a means of deception but these cases are not the norm. Normal policy of not relying on single-source information should help prevent deceptions of this type from being effective."

The counter-deception framework in HUMINT tradecraft involves:
- Never relying on single-source reporting for significant assessments
- Continuously evaluating information for internal consistency and consistency with other-source reporting
- Assessing the source's access, motivation, and opportunity for deception
- Using the A–F / 1–6 rating system honestly — acknowledging "F6" rather than overclaiming reliability for new or unverified sources
- Leveraging CI analysis to detect adversary penetration or manipulation of HUMINT source networks

*(FM 2-22.3; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf); FM 34-60 Counter-HUMINT doctrine; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-60/f34-60_5.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-60/f34-60_5.htm))*

## 8. Strengths and Limitations

### Unique Strength: Intent and Plans-and-Intentions

The most frequently cited doctrinal strength of HUMINT is its unique ability to access adversary intent. The FAS/IOSS Intelligence Collection Activities overview, drawing on IC reference materials, states:

> "Human collectors can provide key insights into the intentions of an adversary, whereas technical collection systems are often limited to determining capabilities. HUMINT can be used to reveal adversary plans and intentions, or uncover scientific and weapons developments before they are used or are detected by technical collection systems."

*(IOSS Overview; available at [https://irp.fas.org/nsa/ioss/threat96/part02.htm](https://irp.fas.org/nsa/ioss/threat96/part02.htm))*

JP 2-01 concurs: "HUMINT and/or SIGINT can capture enemy intent, but GEOINT cannot." This distinction drives prioritization — when the commander's critical information requirement (CCIR) centers on adversary decision-making or planned actions rather than observable physical activity, HUMINT becomes the indispensable discipline.

*(JP 2-01, Chapter III, para. 9.b.(3); available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf))*

### Limitation: Time-Intensive, Slow to Build

HUMINT is among the slowest-developing collection disciplines. Source recruitment and development, trust-building, access validation, and reporting chain establishment take time — often months or years. Source operations require sustained investment before producing reliable reporting. In fast-moving tactical environments, reliance on HUMINT as a primary collection source requires pre-positioned assets and pre-established source networks.

FM 2-22.3 recognizes this in the planning guidance for source operations, noting the sustained nature of HUMINT collector tasking and the need for deliberate operational planning.

*(FM 2-22.3, Chapter 9; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf))*

### Limitation: Source Motivations and Deception Risk

HUMINT depends entirely on human sources — each with their own motivations, biases, limited access, and potential for deception. FM 2-22.3 instructs collectors to assess source "truthfulness, degree of cooperation, and current mood" continuously. Sources may:
- **Fabricate** information (consciously invent) — discredited reports must be formally recalled per DoD policy
- **Exaggerate** their access or the significance of their information
- **Be unwitting vectors of adversary deception** — genuinely reporting information that was planted for them to relay
- **Have selective access** — reporting accurately in one domain while lacking knowledge in others
- **Change over time** — a previously reliable source may develop motivations that compromise reporting quality

The A–F source reliability scale and the 1–6 information credibility scale, combined with the single-source policy, are the primary structural safeguards against these risks.

*(FM 2-22.3; ICD 304; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf))*

### Limitation: Force Protection and OPSEC Burden

HUMINT operations, particularly source operations, impose significant force protection and OPSEC demands. DoDD 5200.37 establishes that Defense HUMINT "activities that raise a significant risk" require DHE approval and USD(I&S) concurrence. OPSEC for HUMINT involves:
- Protecting source identities in IIR source descriptions and context statements
- Preventing adversary CI from identifying HUMINT collection patterns
- Protecting the security of HUMINT personnel operating in hostile environments
- Maintaining source registry security against penetration

ICD 304 states explicitly that the overall HUMINT policy goal is to create an enterprise that "adequately protects sources and methods from compromise."

*(DoDD 5200.37, Section 1.2.h; ICD 304, Section D; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/dni/icd/icd-304.pdf))*

### Limitation: Legal Authority Constraints

Unlike many technical collection disciplines, every HUMINT collection activity requires specific legal authority. DoDD 5200.37 requires that only "trained and certified HUMINT personnel holding the mission and authority" may conduct Defense HUMINT. Activities requiring "sensitive support, or the concealment of true identity or organizational affiliation with assertions of false information, must be approved in accordance with DoDDs S-5205.61 and S-5210.36."

EO 12333 imposes protective requirements for US persons, and the distinction between Title 10 (military operations) and Title 50 (intelligence/covert action) authorities requires ongoing legal review for sensitive source operations.

*(DoDD 5200.37, Section 1.2.i; EO 13470; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm](https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm))*

### Limitation: Coalition and Foreign Disclosure Complexity

In multinational operations, HUMINT information often cannot be shared freely with coalition partners due to source protection requirements. ICD 304 requires dissemination of HUMINT reporting "to the maximum extent possible, with due regard for the protection of sources and methods." DoD Instruction S-5200.42 public portions specify that IIRs "shall be disseminated to maximize release to foreign partners (e.g., Australia, Canada, and the United Kingdom) and to U.S. Federal, State, local, and tribal authorities whenever possible" — but this maximum-release standard is bounded by source protection obligations.

JCS Intelligence Operations doctrine notes that "understanding foreign disclosure and network access processes" is essential and that J2s should "pursue them early" and "invest in and resource the FDO and FDR program."

*(JCS Intelligence Operations Field Paper; available at [https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/fp/intell_ops_fp.pdf](https://www.jcs.mil/Portals/36/Documents/Doctrine/fp/intell_ops_fp.pdf))*

## 9. Cross-Cueing Patterns

Cross-cueing — the use of one intelligence discipline to trigger or focus collection by another — is a fundamental principle of collection management. FM 34-2 (Army Collection Management) states: "Cueing involves the use of one or more sensor systems to provide data that directs collection by other systems... Cueing maximizes the efficient use of finite collection assets in support of multiple, often competing, intelligence collection priorities."

*(FM 34-2, Chapter 3; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm))*

JP 2-01 establishes the cross-cueing principle at the joint level:

> "Tipping and cueing refers to the use of one intelligence discipline, asset, or sensor type to cross-cue or initiate collection by a more precise sensor... Collection disciplines complement each other, and the collection manager should resist favoring or becoming too reliant on a particular sensor, human source, technical system, or technique."

*(JP 2-01, Chapter III; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf))*

FM 2-22.3 notes that a HUMINT collector uses "a variety of collection methods, both passively and actively, to gather information to satisfy the commander's intelligence requirements and **cross-cue other intelligence disciplines**" — acknowledging that cue generation is an explicit HUMINT mission task.

*(FM 2-22.3, para. 1-1; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm2-22-3.pdf))*

### HUMINT → IMINT (Source Tip Cues Imagery Refresh)

A HUMINT source with knowledge of a facility, installation, or activity can provide location, timing, and target description data that enables an imagery collection request against a previously unknown or underutilized target. The collection manager can use a low-level HUMINT tip to cue a UAV or satellite pass — FM 34-2 provides the specific example: "you plan to use a low-level HUMINT source 24 hours prior to UAV launch to confirm or deny activity along a key corridor."

When a source reports activity at a specific location — a construction site, vehicle park, or personnel movement — the J-2 can task imagery collectors to capture and analyze that location, confirming, denying, or expanding on the HUMINT report. This pattern is especially valuable when HUMINT can provide specific time-windows for activity, enabling tasking against dynamic targets.

*(FM 34-2, Chapter 3; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm))*

### HUMINT → SIGINT (Source Identifies Communications Nodes / Personalities)

A HUMINT source can identify:
- **Communications nodes**: specific frequencies, call signs, encryption procedures, or network architectures used by adversary organizations
- **Named individuals** associated with specific communications devices or accounts
- **Meeting times and patterns** when adversary leadership communicates in predictable ways
- **Physical locations** of communications infrastructure

This information enables SIGINT collectors to focus collection against previously unknown or difficult-to-locate adversary communications, dramatically improving collection efficiency. FM 34-2 describes this complementary pattern: "scouts report resupply activity within a known assembly area; SIGINT intercept of the associated logistics net provides unit identity, subordination, and indications of future activity." The reverse flow (HUMINT cues SIGINT) is the leading sequence when HUMINT identifies the target set.

*(FM 34-2, Chapter 3; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm))*

### SIGINT → HUMINT (Intercept Identifies Recruitment Opportunity — Lawful Only)

A SIGINT intercept may reveal a foreign individual who is disaffected with their government, has access to valuable information, or is communicating in ways that suggest willingness to cooperate with US interests. This information can cue a HUMINT element to initiate contact — through overt or liaison channels — with that individual as a potential recruitment target.

This pattern requires careful legal management. EO 12333 constrains how SIGINT-derived information can be used to task HUMINT collection, particularly involving US persons. ICD 310 governs the coordination of clandestine human source activities outside the US, and any SIGINT-to-HUMINT cueing that involves initiating contact with a foreign national based on intercepted communications requires review within established deconfliction procedures.

*(EO 13470, Section 1.3(b)(20); ICD 310; available at [https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm](https://irp.fas.org/offdocs/eo/eo-13470.htm) and [https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD-310-Coord-of-Clandestine-Human-and-Human-enabled-FI-and-CI-outside-the-US-2016-06-27.pdf](https://www.dni.gov/files/documents/ICD/ICD-310-Coord-of-Clandestine-Human-and-Human-enabled-FI-and-CI-outside-the-US-2016-06-27.pdf))*

### IMINT → HUMINT (Imagery Anomaly Cues Source Tasking)

An imagery analyst who identifies an anomaly — a new construction feature, unusual vehicle concentration, unexplained activity pattern, or infrastructure change — can generate a collection requirement tasked to HUMINT to identify the purpose of the activity. The imagery discipline can detect *that* something is happening; only HUMINT may be able to determine *why*.

This is a classic application of the capability / intent distinction: GEOINT identifies the physical manifestation; HUMINT determines the intent behind it. The J-2X translates the IMINT anomaly into a HUMINT tasking — a Source-Directed Requirement (SDR) or RFI — and tasks available HUMINT assets to provide explanation.

*(JP 2-01, Chapter III; FM 34-2, Chapter 3; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/jp2_01.pdf) and [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-2/Ch3.htm))*

### HUMINT → CI (CI Screening of Sources)

Every HUMINT source is also a potential CI concern. HUMINT source operations are a primary vector for adversary counter-intelligence: foreign intelligence services may attempt to recruit or manipulate US HUMINT sources, introduce double agents, or identify US HUMINT personnel through their sources. FM 34-60 and MCWP 2-14 both note that CI and HUMINT work "hand-in-hand":

> "HUMINT is the intelligence activity that has the most important connective tie to CI. CI and HUMINT work hand-in-hand because of the nature of their targets and the type of intelligence missions they perform: CI neutralizing the enemy intelligence effort and HUMINT collecting information about enemy activity."

*(MCWP 2-14, para. 6002; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/usmc/mcwp2-14.pdf))*

The cross-cue from HUMINT to CI operates continuously: HUMINT collectors report observations about sources that may indicate CI concerns (unusual questions from the source, potential surveillance, anomalous behavior), and CI elements use that information to assess adversary penetration or double-agent operations. DoDD 5200.37 requires that the J2X coordinate and deconflict all HUMINT and CI collection, ensuring that the CI screening function is integrated with, not separate from, source management.

Counter-HUMINT (C-HUMINT) analysis, described in FM 34-60 Chapter 5, focuses on identifying adversary HUMINT collection activities, neutralizing or exploiting them, and protecting friendly information from adversary human sources — completing the CI-HUMINT integration loop.

*(FM 34-60, Chapter 5; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-60/f34-60_5.htm](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/army/fm34-60/f34-60_5.htm); DoDD 5200.37; available at [https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf](https://irp.fas.org/doddir/dod/d5200_37.pdf))*
